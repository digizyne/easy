# Chapter 18: When Leadership Pushes Back

The engineers on a team often warm to EASY quickly. The objections tend to come from above. Middle managers who have built their professional identity around sprint ceremonies, VPs who present velocity charts to the board, executives who equate process complexity with organizational maturity: these are the people who will ask hard questions when EASY is proposed, and they deserve direct answers rather than reassurances.

This chapter addresses the objections that come up in almost every leadership conversation about EASY. They are not unreasonable objections. Most of them reflect real concerns about accountability, predictability, and visibility that any responsible leader would raise. The answers are simply different from what traditional Agile practice has taught people to expect.

*"How Will We Know Who's Accountable?"*

This is the most common objection, and it conflates two things that do not have to be the same: accountability for outcomes and tracking of task assignments.

In a traditional Agile setup, the assignee field does accountability-tracking work. Remove the assignee field and people assume accountability disappears with it. It does not. Accountability for outcomes shifts to the team as the unit of delivery and to the team lead as the person responsible for the team's output. If a feature ships and works, the team is accountable for it. If it does not ship, the team lead is accountable for explaining why and for resolving the obstacle.

This is cleaner accountability than the assignee model, not weaker. When a sprint misses its targets under Scrum, the retrospective tends to become a negotiation about:

- Which tickets got underestimated
- Which developers were pulled into meetings
- Which dependencies were not accounted for in planning

The accountability is spread thin enough that no one owns the failure in any meaningful sense. **Under EASY, the team shipped or it did not, and that fact is visible to everyone.** There is nowhere for the accountability to hide.

*"How Do We Plan Capacity?"*

The honest answer is that capacity planning in the traditional sense is no longer reliable regardless of which methodology you use, for reasons Chapter 2 covered in detail. AI has destabilized the relationship between person-hours and output in ways that capacity planning has no mechanism to accommodate. The question is not whether to abandon capacity planning under EASY; the question is whether capacity planning was producing reliable guidance before EASY.

For organizations with genuine external commitments tied to delivery timelines, such as:

- Contracted deliverables
- Regulatory deadlines
- Customer agreements

The answer is to plan at the feature level, not the task level. The question is not "how many story points can the team complete in Q3" but "which of these features are essential by which dates, and is our current rate of delivery consistent with meeting them." The Done column gives a clear, real-time answer to the second question without requiring any estimation apparatus to maintain it.

*"How Do We Know If We're On Track?"*

Look at the board. Count the cards in Done. Ask whether the team is finishing the right things in a time frame consistent with the organization's goals.

This answer frustrates leaders who are accustomed to burndown charts and velocity trend lines. Those tools create the feeling of precision: a number, a trend, a projected completion date. But as Chapter 5 argued, they largely measure how well a team has learned to match its estimates to its completions, not how productive the team actually is. **Exchanging false precision for honest visibility is not a loss, it's a correction.**

If leadership needs more granularity than the board provides, the right instrument is a conversation with the team lead, not a more complex dashboard. A team lead who cannot tell you whether the team is on track without consulting a velocity chart has a different problem than a methodology problem.

*"Does This Scale?"*

EASY scales as well as any other methodology for the simple reason that the constraints it imposes are minimal. Large organizations running multiple EASY teams coordinate between them through:

- Shared goals and product ownership
- Clear ownership of product areas
- Regular leadership communication about priorities

The board structure does not change at scale. The card format does not change. The challenge of coordinating across teams is a leadership and organizational design challenge, not a methodology challenge.

What EASY does not do is provide a scaling framework like SAFe or LeSS. If your organization requires an elaborate cross-team ceremony structure to coordinate, EASY does not prohibit it. **What it does insist on is that the team-level unit of tracking remains simple: a card, and two columns**.

*When the Argument Needs an Outside Voice*

Internal advocates for EASY often find that the arguments in this chapter land differently coming from them than they would coming from an outside expert. This is not a failure of the argument. It is a feature of how organizations evaluate new ideas. A recommendation from inside the organization carries the weight of internal politics. The same recommendation from a recognized practitioner of the methodology carries different authority.

Certified EASY consultants are trained specifically to facilitate executive conversations about the methodology. If your organization's leadership is skeptical and an internal advocate has not been able to move the conversation forward, bringing in a consultant for a half-day leadership session is often the most efficient path to a genuine decision, yes or no, rather than continued deferral.
