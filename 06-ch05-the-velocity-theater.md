# Chapter 5: The Velocity Theater

There is a number that appears on the dashboard of nearly every Scrum team in the world, updated at the end of every sprint, discussed in planning meetings and retrospectives, used to project roadmaps and communicate progress to stakeholders. That number is velocity: the count of story points completed in a given sprint. It carries the authority of a metric. It is displayed on charts. It has a trend line. Leaders look at it and form opinions about their teams.

**Velocity is not a productivity metric. It is a consistency metric, and the thing it measures most reliably is how well a team has learned to calibrate its estimates to match its output.** Understanding this distinction matters, because organizations routinely treat velocity as though it measures the first thing when it only ever measured the second.

Here is what velocity actually captures. A team estimates tasks in story points at the beginning of a sprint. At the end of the sprint, they count the points on the completed tasks. The ratio between what they estimated and what they finished is implicitly what velocity tracks over time. A team with stable velocity is not necessarily a productive team. It is a team that has gotten good at estimating roughly as many points as it can complete, which is a form of skill, but not the form that ships products. A team can have perfectly stable velocity while shipping almost nothing of value, as long as it sizes its tickets carefully and completes them consistently.

The economist Charles Goodhart articulated the relevant principle in 1975: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.[^1] The moment velocity became the number that managers watched and used to evaluate team health, it became a number that teams learned to manage. This does not require any deliberate dishonesty. It happens naturally, through the accumulated small decisions of developers trying to avoid looking bad. Tasks that are nearly done at the end of a sprint get pulled in from the next sprint's backlog to pad the count. Tasks that look risky get estimated higher so there is buffer to absorb the uncertainty. Large tasks get broken into smaller pieces not because smaller pieces are better for the work, but because smaller pieces are easier to complete within a sprint boundary and therefore safer for the velocity number.

The result is that a team's velocity tells you very little about how much value the team is producing or how efficiently it is working. It tells you mainly how long the team has been practicing Scrum, since stable velocity is almost entirely a product of experience with the estimation and completion cycle rather than a product of genuine productivity improvement.

**What velocity actually motivates is the performance of productivity rather than productivity itself.** Sprint reviews become presentations. Burndown charts become props. The ceremonies that were designed to create accountability for real work become rituals for demonstrating that the number is moving in the right direction. Anyone who has sat through a sprint review where a team presented a collection of small tickets closed and called it a successful sprint will recognize the dynamic. The work done may or may not be the most valuable work the team could have done. The velocity number provides no information either way.

This problem predates AI, and some practitioners have been pointing it out for years. The response from the Agile community has generally been to recommend better estimation practices, more rigorous story point calibration, and team-level velocity rather than individual velocity to discourage gaming. These are reasonable suggestions that address the symptom rather than the disease. The disease is that velocity is a circular metric: teams estimate tasks, complete tasks, and then measure how closely their completions matched their estimates, which they then use to plan how many estimates to make next sprint. The information flowing through this loop tells you nothing about the outside world.

When AI enters the picture, the circularity becomes even more pronounced. Chapter 2 described how AI makes velocity unstable by introducing sudden, unpredictable changes in throughput. What is worth adding here is that it also makes the performance aspect of velocity more demanding and less meaningful simultaneously. Teams now face the task of producing consistent velocity numbers from a production process that is genuinely inconsistent, because AI assistance varies dramatically based on factors that have nothing to do with the team's planning or effort. The response, in teams that are paying attention, is more sophisticated gaming: larger buffers, more conservative estimates, deliberate under-commitment so that the velocity line stays smooth regardless of what actually happened during the sprint.

The sprint review where everyone looks at the burndown chart and nods has always had some theater in it. In the age of AI, the theater has become more elaborate and the signal has become weaker. The team is now performing consistency on top of genuinely unpredictable output, and the performance takes real time and real cognitive energy that could be spent on the work.

EASY removes the metric entirely, which removes the performance incentive along with it. There is no velocity to manage, no chart to update, no trend line for anyone to form opinions about. There are cards that are done and cards that are not done, and the only question worth asking is whether the done pile is growing with the things that matter most at the top. **A team that is working on the right things and finishing them does not need a velocity number to prove it. The shipped work is the proof, and it speaks more clearly than any chart.**

---

[^1]: Goodhart, Charles. "Problems of Monetary Management: The U.K. Experience." In *Papers in Monetary Economics*, vol. 1. Sydney: Reserve Bank of Australia, 1975. The principle is often paraphrased as "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure," though Goodhart's original formulation was specific to monetary policy. The generalized version was popularized by Marilyn Strathern in 1997 and has since been validated across a wide range of organizational and behavioral contexts.
