# Chapter 16: Hiring for EASY

The trust infrastructure described in the previous chapter does not build itself. It is built, primarily, through hiring. The people on a team determine whether the team can function without ceremony, and no process can compensate for the wrong people in the room. This has always been true. What has changed is what "the right people" looks like in an AI-augmented environment running EASY.

*What You're Actually Hiring For*

Traditional software hiring is organized around technical skill: does this person know the language, the framework, the architecture pattern? The implicit model is that skill produces output, and output is what you need. This was a reasonable proxy when developers were the primary source of implementation throughput.

That model is breaking down. AI coding assistants are capable of generating correct implementations of many software tasks when given adequate context and direction. **What AI cannot do is decide what to build, evaluate whether the output is good, determine when a card is genuinely done, or identify the problems that no one has articulated yet.** Those capabilities live entirely in the human on the other side of the prompt. And they are not reliably correlated with raw coding skill.

What an EASY team actually needs from its people is a different set of qualities:

- **Judgment**: the ability to evaluate AI-generated output critically, to know when something is good enough and when it needs revision, to catch the subtle errors that pass automated tests
- **Taste**: strong opinions about what good software looks like, what is maintainable and what isn't, what solves the actual problem as opposed to what merely closes the ticket
- **Initiative**: the ability to identify what matters most and pursue it without being directed task by task
- **Honesty**: the willingness to say "this is blocked," "I don't understand this," or "this isn't done yet" without social pressure forcing the answer in a more comfortable direction

These qualities exist in developers of varying technical backgrounds. They are not reliably signaled by a résumé full of the right technologies or years of experience with a particular Agile framework.

*Reading the Signals*

The challenge is that standard hiring processes are better designed to assess technical skill than these deeper qualities. Coding challenges and whiteboard exercises assess implementation ability. They tell you almost nothing about whether someone has judgment, taste, or initiative. To assess those qualities, you have to look elsewhere:

- **How they talk about past work**: candidates who describe outcomes ("we shipped a feature that reduced checkout abandonment by 20%") are demonstrating outcome orientation. Candidates who describe activity ("I was responsible for the payment service") are demonstrating task orientation. In an EASY environment, you need the former.
- **How they handle ambiguity**: give candidates an underspecified problem and watch how they respond. Do they ask clarifying questions about the goal, or do they immediately start solving for the most obvious interpretation? The ability to identify what a card actually needs to accomplish, rather than what it literally says, is essential.
- **What they are critical of**: candidates with genuine taste will have strong opinions about code quality, system design, and what makes software good or bad.
- **Their relationship with process**: ask about the best and worst process environments they have worked in. Someone who thrives without structure will describe it clearly. Someone who needs external scaffolding to be productive will reveal that too.

*A Note on Certification*

As the EASY methodology has grown, a formal certification track has developed through the EASY Institute for practitioners who want to demonstrate fluency in the methodology and its underlying principles. **EASY certification is not a substitute for judgment, taste, or initiative, but it is a meaningful signal that a practitioner has internalized the philosophy and has been validated on its application in real team environments.**

For organizations hiring for EASY-native roles, certification provides a useful filter. For individual practitioners, it provides a credential that distinguishes them in a market that is still largely organized around traditional Agile frameworks. For consulting engagements, it is the standard by which EASY practitioners are recognized as qualified to guide teams through the transition.

Certification details, including program requirements and the current roster of certified practitioners, are available through the EASY Institute. If you are building a team and want to hire people who already understand this methodology deeply, that roster is a reasonable place to start.
