# Chapter 13: AI Is a Team Member

Every project management system ever built has assumed something so obvious about its users that it was never worth writing down: the people doing the work are people. This seems almost too trivial to mention. Of course they are. Who else would they be?

The answer, increasingly, is that a meaningful portion of the implementation work being done on software teams is being done by AI tools, and this fact is straining every management system that was built around the unexamined assumption of an all-human workforce. Standups ask "what did you work on yesterday?" Retrospectives ask "how is the team feeling?" Capacity planning asks "how many person-days do we have?" Assignee fields ask "which human is responsible for this?" These questions make sense when the workforce is composed entirely of humans. They begin to feel strange when a substantial portion of the work is being completed by a tool that has no feelings about the sprint, doesn't get fatigued, can't attend the standup, and doesn't need a ticket assigned to it in order to get started.

Traditional Agile has no model for this. The Agile Manifesto was written by and for human practitioners, and its values, admirable as they are, reflect a world in which the labor being coordinated was always human labor. The phrase "individuals and interactions" meant individuals in the literal sense: people with names, working hours, skill levels, and communication styles that a good process would accommodate. The manifesto did not anticipate a world in which one of those individuals might be a language model generating five hundred lines of tested, documented code in the time it takes its human colleagues to finish a sprint planning discussion.

EASY was not designed with AI in mind as a curiosity or a future consideration. AI is simply part of the reality that EASY is honest about. **The card describes a unit of work. It does not describe who will do that work, how long it will take, or what tools will be involved.** Those details are irrelevant to the system, not because they don't matter in practice but because they are not the system's business. A card exists to capture an intent: this thing needs to exist and it does not yet. Whether a human writes it from scratch, an AI generates it in its entirety, or the outcome is some collaborative combination of the two, the card's purpose is unchanged and its movement from Not Done to Done is equally meaningful.

*What AI Actually Does*

To understand why EASY accommodates AI naturally while traditional Agile does not, it helps to be concrete about what AI contribution to software development actually looks like in practice today.

A developer working on a feature will often begin by describing the problem to an AI coding assistant. They might describe the desired behavior, the technical constraints, and any relevant context about the existing codebase. The AI generates an implementation, sometimes a complete one, sometimes a partial one that the developer then modifies. The developer evaluates the output, tests it against the actual requirements, revises it where the AI's interpretation was off, and iterates until the implementation is correct and meets whatever quality standard the team holds. In many cases the AI will also generate the unit tests, suggest edge cases the developer hadn't considered, and produce a first draft of any documentation the feature requires.[^1]

This process is not a developer passively accepting AI output. It is a collaboration in which the human provides judgment, direction, and evaluation while the AI provides implementation throughput. The developer's contribution is real and important. But it is categorically different from the contribution that Agile's metrics and ceremonies were designed to track. Story points estimate the effort of writing code. They do not estimate the effort of directing an AI to write code and then verifying the output. Sprint velocity measures how much implementation work a team completes per cycle. It does not measure how much judgment they exercised. T**hese distinctions matter, because the thing that is now scarce and valuable on a software team is not implementation capacity; it is the human judgment to know what to build, how to evaluate the result, and when it is good enough**.

*The One Question AI Cannot Answer*

There is a question at the center of every software project that AI has not made easier to answer and shows no signs of making easier anytime soon: is this done? Not done in a technical sense, not "does the code compile and do the tests pass," but done in the sense of meeting the actual human need that the card was written to address. Does this feature work the way a real user would expect it to work? Is this implementation maintainable enough that the team won't regret it in six months? Does this solve the problem that was originally identified, or did we build a technically correct solution to the wrong problem?

These questions require human judgment. They require someone who understands the context, the users, the business, and the standards the team has set for itself. AI can produce output at remarkable speed, but it cannot evaluate whether its own output is good enough by the criteria that actually matter. **That evaluation is what moves a card from Not Done to Done, and it is the one function of a project management system that AI has not made redundant and likely cannot.** EASY is structured around this fact. The entire system exists to track which human judgments have been made, specifically, has a human determined that this work meets the standard and declared it done?

Traditional Agile, by contrast, tracks a number of things that AI has undermined. It tracks who is doing the work (the assignee), but AI has blurred authorship to the point where this is often misleading. It tracks how much effort the work will require (the estimate), but AI has decoupled effort from time in ways that estimation cannot accommodate. It tracks velocity over time, but AI-assisted velocity is not comparable to pre-AI velocity in any useful way. The things Agile was built to track have become difficult or impossible to track accurately. The one thing EASY tracks, done or not done, has become more important than ever.

*Common Objections*

When teams first hear that EASY treats AI as a natural participant in the work rather than a novelty to be managed separately, a reasonable concern sometimes surfaces: if the system doesn't distinguish between human work and AI work, won't that obscure whether the team is actually developing their skills and understanding the codebase?

The answer to this is simple: EASY is a project management methodology. It is not a hiring framework, a learning and development program, or a code review standard. The concern about whether developers are genuinely learning and maintaining deep understanding of the systems they build is real, but it is not a problem that any project management system has ever been well positioned to solve. Scrum does not ensure that developers understand their codebase. Kanban does not ensure it either. The practices that address this concern are code review, pair programming, documentation standards, and the kind of team culture that values understanding over output. Those practices are compatible with EASY and should be pursued alongside it.

**What EASY does ensure is that the question of whether work is done gets answered by a human who has sufficient context and judgment to answer it correctly.** That human, in evaluating whether a card can move to Done, is necessarily engaging with the substance of the work. They are asking whether it works, whether it meets the standard, and whether it is genuinely complete. In an AI-augmented team, this evaluation is where the human expertise most concentrates, and it is the function that the EASY board is designed to make visible and unambiguous.

A card moves to Done when a human says it does. AI may have written the code. AI may have written the tests and the documentation. But a human decided the work was finished, and the board shows that decision clearly. That is what EASY tracks, and in a world of AI-accelerated implementation, it is what most needs tracking.
