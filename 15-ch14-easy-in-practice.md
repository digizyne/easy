# Chapter 14: EASY in Practice

The best way to understand what EASY feels like is to contrast it with what most software teams experience today. Consider two teams building the same product. Both are competent. Both have access to the same AI tools. The difference is their process.

*The Traditional Agile Team*

The day starts with a standup. Fifteen minutes on paper, thirty-five in practice. Each developer reports on what they did yesterday, what they plan to do today, and whether anything is blocking them. Half of this information is already visible in Jira. The other half will be outdated by noon. After standup, two developers are pulled into a backlog grooming session to estimate next sprint's tickets. They spend ninety minutes debating whether a feature is a five-point story or an eight. They land on five. It will take four hours with AI assistance. No one updates the estimate.

By early afternoon, the team has collectively spent more time in coordination than in work. A developer who finished their task before lunch moved the Jira ticket through three columns, wrote a status comment, and tagged a colleague for review. The colleague acknowledges the tag and adds it to their queue. The ticket sits.

*The EASY Team*

The day starts with the board. Not a meeting about the board. The board itself. Each developer looks at the Not Done column, picks a card from it, and starts working. There is no ceremony to begin. The work is visible. The priorities are clear. Picking up the next available card is the whole process.

A developer opens their AI coding assistant, describes the feature on their card, reviews and refines the output, runs the tests, and ships. They move the card to Done. It took three hours. They pick up the next card.

**At end of day, the team does not file status updates or move tickets through intermediate columns. The Done column tells the story**. Ten cards moved today. Here is what they represent.

*What This Looks Like at Scale*

The difference compounds. A traditional Agile team running two-week sprints will hold, at minimum, the following recurring ceremonies:

- Daily standup (10 meetings per sprint)
- Sprint planning (one two-to-four hour session)
- Sprint review (one hour)
- Sprint retrospective (one hour)
- Backlog grooming (one to two hours per week)

That is conservatively four to six hours per developer per sprint spent in process ceremonies, before accounting for the time required to maintain ticket metadata, write status updates, and navigate the tooling.[^1] For a team of eight, that is thirty-two to forty-eight engineering hours per sprint consumed by process rather than product.

An EASY team has none of these ceremonies by default. If the team wants to sync, they sync. If there is a problem to discuss, they discuss it. The methodology does not mandate any of it.

*The Role of AI in an EASY Day*

EASY and AI tools are not just compatible; they are natural partners. AI compresses implementation time to the point where the traditional sprint cadence makes no sense. A card that represents two days of work without AI assistance might represent four hours with it. In an EASY team, that card gets picked up, completed, and moved to Done in a single session. There is no "in progress" state to track. There is no mid-sprint status to report. The card is done, and the next one is ready.

This is the rhythm that EASY is designed to support: pull, work, finish, repeat. AI accelerates every step except the last judgment call, which belongs to the human who decides the work meets the standard and moves the card.

*What Leadership Sees*

Executives and managers often worry that removing process ceremonies removes visibility. In practice, the opposite is true. A Done column full of completed cards is more informative than a Jira board full of tickets in various states of "in progress," "in review," and "blocked." The EASY board answers the only question leadership actually needs answered: is work getting done?

The answer is either yes or no. If yes, the Done column grows. If no, the team has a conversation about why. No report needed. No dashboard required. The board is the report.
