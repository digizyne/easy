# Chapter 17: The EASY Rollout

The transition to EASY is not technically complicated. There is no new software to learn, no certification required before you start, and no multi-month implementation program. The board has two columns. The cards have one field. You can be running EASY by end of day.

What is complicated is the cultural transition, and rushing it is the most reliable way to fail. Teams that try to flip from Scrum to EASY overnight tend to experience a disorienting few weeks in which the process scaffolding is gone but the habits it supported are still very much in place. People wait to be assigned work. Cards accumulate in Not Done without anyone pulling them. Standup gets cancelled but no one is sure how to stay coordinated. The board looks simple and the team feels lost.

The sequence below is designed to avoid that. It is a deliberate, phased transition that removes process overhead incrementally while building the trust behaviors that EASY requires.

*Start with One Team*

Do not roll EASY out organization-wide on the first attempt. Choose one team: ideally a small one, ideally one where the team lead is already skeptical of ceremony overhead and the members are self-directed enough to function without constant coordination. Run a thirty-day pilot. Track what ships. At the end of thirty days, evaluate honestly: did the team produce more, less, or roughly the same amount of useful work? Was the team more or less stressed? What did they miss from the old process, and was what they missed actually valuable?

The pilot serves two purposes. It gives the methodology a fair test in a controlled environment, and it produces a concrete case study to present to the rest of the organization when the broader rollout begins.

*The Rollout Sequence*

Once the pilot team is ready, the transition follows a deliberate sequence:

1. **Set up the board.** Create the Not Done and Done columns. Migrate existing in-flight work as cards. Strip every card down to a description of the work, removing assignees, due dates, story points, labels, and sprint associations. If a card cannot be described as an outcome without its metadata, rewrite it until it can.
2. **Kill the daily standup.** Replace it with nothing, initially. The board is the standup. Anyone who needs to know what the team is working on can look at the board. If the team feels the need to sync, they can sync. Make it optional. Watch whether anyone calls a sync meeting and whether it was useful when they did.
3. **Cancel the next sprint planning session.** Do not plan the next sprint. Pull from the backlog in priority order. If priority is unclear, that is a conversation for the team lead and the product owner, not a two-hour planning poker session.
4. **Let the sprint end without a review.** Replace the sprint review with a standing update: here is what moved to Done this week, here is what is at the top of Not Done. Send it in writing. Skip the slide deck.
5. **Cancel the retrospective.** If there is something worth discussing, discuss it. Do not schedule a recurring meeting to discuss it on a fixed cadence regardless of whether anything worth discussing has happened.
6. **Repeat.** After thirty days, assess. Most teams find that the first few things they miss from the old process were not valuable once they were gone. The few things they genuinely miss are worth reinstating selectively.

*The Official EASY Board*

Teams making this transition often ask whether they should use Jira, Trello, or another existing tool configured to approximate the EASY board. The honest answer is that it is possible to run EASY on almost any board tool by simply ignoring most of its features. But ignoring features requires discipline, and over time most teams drift back toward using what the tool makes easy.

The official EASY board, available through the EASY Institute, was built specifically to enforce the methodology's constraints by design. It has two columns and no mechanism for adding a third. Cards have a single description field and nothing else. There are no assignees, no due dates, no custom states, and no settings that introduce them. **The tool cannot be configured to violate the methodology, which means compliance is a consequence of using it rather than a discipline imposed on top of it.**

The EASY board is available in a free tier suitable for small teams and individual practitioners, and in paid tiers with collaboration and administration features designed for larger organizations. For teams running the pilot described above, the free tier is sufficient to get started.

*When to Bring in Help*

Most teams can run this rollout without outside assistance. The sequence is straightforward, and the methodology itself is simple enough that a team lead who has read this book thoroughly has everything needed to facilitate the transition.

Some organizations cannot do it alone for various reasons: 
- An engineering culture that is deeply invested in the existing process
- A leadership team that requires more than an internal advocate to take the methodology seriously
- A rollout that needs to happen across many teams simultaneously
- A pilot that is struggling for reasons the team cannot clearly diagnose.

For these situations, certified EASY consultants are available through the EASY Institute. A consultant's role in a rollout is not to run the process for the team but to guide the transition, coach the team lead, facilitate the conversations that tend to get stuck, and provide an outside perspective on what is working and what is not. Consulting engagements typically run four to eight weeks for a single-team rollout and longer for organization-wide transitions.

The right time to bring in a consultant is before the rollout, not after it has failed. A good consultant will compress the timeline, reduce the friction, and leave the team with habits that sustain the methodology without ongoing support.
