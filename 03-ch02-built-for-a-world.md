# Chapter 2: Built for a World That No Longer Exists

Every methodology is a set of assumptions wearing a costume. The assumptions come first; the practices, ceremonies, and tools come later, designed to serve those assumptions. If you want to understand why a methodology works or doesn't work, you have to look past the practices to the assumptions underneath them. And if the assumptions are wrong, no amount of fine-tuning the practices will fix the problem.

Agile was built on a specific set of assumptions about the nature of software work, and for most of the methodology's history, those assumptions were reasonable. They were so reasonable, in fact, that most practitioners never had to examine them. The assumptions were simply true of the world, and so the practices built on top of them seemed naturally correct. It is only now, as the underlying reality shifts, that the assumptions become visible, in the same way that you only notice the frame of a window when the glass breaks.

The foundational assumption of Agile, the one that every other practice rests on, is this: software is built by human beings, working at human speed, with human limitations, and the central challenge of software development is coordinating those human beings effectively.

**This assumption is not wrong. It is just incomplete in ways that now matter enormously**. Consider story points, the unit of estimation that Scrum and its variants use to measure the size and complexity of work. A story point is not a unit of time, as any Scrum practitioner will quickly tell you. It is a unit of relative complexity, intended to capture how difficult a piece of work is compared to other pieces of work the team has done before. Teams assign story points through a process called planning poker, in which each team member votes on the complexity of a given task, and the group discusses and reconciles their estimates.

The entire system rests on a premise that most people who use it have never had to state aloud: that the relevant variable in measuring work complexity is human cognitive effort. A five-point story is harder for a human being to complete than a two-point story. A thirteen-point story is complex enough that a human team should consider breaking it into smaller pieces. The scale reflects the distribution of human effort across tasks of varying difficulty.

Now introduce an AI coding assistant into the picture. A task that a senior developer would rate as an eight-point story, perhaps because it requires careful research, reading unfamiliar code, writing a non-trivial algorithm, and then testing it against edge cases, might be reduced to a two-hour effort with AI handling the research, the implementation skeleton, and the initial test generation. The human being's role shifts from doing the work to directing and reviewing it. The cognitive effort involved changes in kind, not just in degree.

Your story point scale was not built for this. It was calibrated against a baseline of human effort that no longer applies consistently, and it will be different for every developer depending on how effectively they use AI tools. Two developers on the same team, given the same ticket, can now have wildly different actual completion times based on nothing more than their proficiency with their AI tooling. **The estimate has become less a measure of task complexity and more a measure of individual tooling skill, which was never what it was supposed to measure.**

Velocity, the metric that tracks how many story points a team completes per sprint, has the same problem compounded over time. Velocity was always meant to serve as a planning tool: if a team consistently completes around forty story points per sprint, a manager can project forward with some confidence about what the team will deliver over the next quarter. The underlying assumption is that a team's throughput is relatively stable from sprint to sprint, because it is primarily a function of team size and composition, which change slowly.

**AI destroys this stability**. A new AI tool, a better prompt strategy, a more capable model release, any of these can change a team's effective throughput significantly and suddenly. A team that averaged forty story points per sprint might complete seventy in the sprint after half the team starts using a new AI coding assistant, not because the team got larger or more experienced, but because their tools improved. Historical velocity data becomes unreliable as a planning baseline the moment AI becomes a meaningful contributor to output, because the tool's capability is not constant in the way human skill roughly is.

There is also the more fundamental question of what velocity is actually measuring in a world where AI contributes substantially to output. If a developer completes sixty story points in a sprint largely because an AI tool generated the code that those sixty points represent, is that a measure of the developer's velocity or the AI's? The metric was designed to measure human team throughput. It is now measuring something more ambiguous, and planning decisions made from it are therefore less reliable than they appear.

Capacity planning, the practice of looking at a team's available person-hours in a given sprint and comparing that figure to the estimated cost of the work being planned, has a similar structural problem. Capacity planning treats time as the scarce resource and story points as the unit of cost. You have ten developers, each available for eight hours a day over a two-week sprint, giving you a theoretical capacity of eight hundred person-hours, minus meetings, minus holidays, minus whatever percentage of time experience tells you is lost to context switching and overhead.

This model assumes that the rate at which work gets done is roughly proportional to the number of person-hours applied to it. More people, more hours, more output. It is a fundamentally industrial model of work: inputs of labor produce outputs of features at a predictable ratio.

AI has broken the linearity of that ratio in ways capacity planning has no mechanism to accommodate. The relationship between person-hours and output is no longer stable or predictable from historical data. A team of five developers using AI tools effectively can outproduce a team of ten developers who are not, and the same five-person team might triple their output next quarter when a more capable AI model becomes available, without any change to their headcount, their hours, or their process.

**Planning against capacity, in this environment, gives the appearance of rigor while resting on a premise that is increasingly fictional**. You are calculating with numbers that no longer mean what they meant when your planning system was designed.

The sprint itself, the fixed-length iteration that is the basic unit of Scrum-based planning, was designed around the idea that meaningful, shippable software takes roughly two weeks of human effort to produce. The two-week sprint is not an arbitrary number. It reflects a considered judgment about the right balance between planning overhead and delivery cadence: short enough to allow frequent feedback and course correction, long enough to produce something worth showing to a customer.

AI compresses the time dimension of software work in ways the sprint structure cannot absorb gracefully. When a feature that previously required two weeks of focused development can be produced in three days, the sprint becomes a waiting room. The work is done, but the team holds it until the sprint review because the process requires it. **The two-week cadence that was calibrated to human throughput is now an artificial constraint on a process that could move significantly faster.**

Some teams respond to this by filling the remaining sprint time with additional work, which produces its own distortions: rushed scope expansion, poorly considered additions, and a sprint review that covers far more ground than the team can discuss meaningfully. Others hold to the sprint boundary and accept the resulting idle time as a cost of process compliance. Neither response addresses the underlying issue, which is that the sprint length was chosen to match a pace of work that no longer describes how the best teams operate.

None of this is an argument that the people who designed these practices were careless or that the practices themselves were poorly conceived. Story points, velocity, capacity planning, and the sprint structure were all thoughtful responses to real problems that software teams faced. They represented genuine progress over the waterfall approaches they replaced.

**The argument is simpler and less comforting: they were designed for a specific world, and that world is changing faster than the practices are**. Every one of these tools was built around the assumption that human cognitive effort is the primary input to software development, that it can be estimated with reasonable accuracy, and that historical data about that effort is a reliable guide to future planning. AI is eroding each of those assumptions simultaneously, and the erosion is not a distant concern. It is happening now, in organizations that are still running two-week sprints and holding planning poker sessions as if the rules of the game haven't changed.

A 2023 study by GitHub found that developers using GitHub Copilot completed coding tasks 55% faster than those working without it.[^1] A McKinsey analysis from the same year estimated that AI tools could accelerate certain software development tasks by 20 to 45 percent.[^2] These figures will look conservative within a few years. The direction of travel is clear, and the gap between what these tools make possible and what our processes are designed to handle is widening every quarter.

The rest of Part One examines the specific practices that break most visibly under these conditions, starting with the one whose failure is the most philosophically interesting: the assignee.
