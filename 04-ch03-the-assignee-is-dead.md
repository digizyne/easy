# Chapter 3: The Assignee Is Dead

Ask a Scrum practitioner why every ticket needs an assignee, and you will get a reasonable answer. Accountability requires that someone own each piece of work. If no one is named on a task, no one feels responsible for it, and tasks without owners have a way of sitting quietly in a backlog until someone notices they're overdue. The assignee field is how teams ensure that every unit of work has a human being attached to it, someone whose name will appear in the sprint review, someone who will be asked about it in standup. It is the mechanism by which the team's collective responsibility for the work gets divided into individual obligations.

This logic is not wrong. It is built on a genuine insight about human behavior: diffuse responsibility tends to produce diffuse effort. When everyone owns something, no one does. The assignee field was a practical solution to a real coordination problem, and for decades it worked adequately for the purpose it served.

**The problem is not that accountability has become less important. The problem is that the assignee field was never really about accountability in the first place. It was about tracking human labor.** The person assigned to a ticket was the person doing the work, and naming them accomplished two things simultaneously: it established responsibility and it tracked effort. These two functions were so naturally bundled together that most teams never had to think about them as separate things. Assigning work to a person meant that person would do it, and therefore knowing who was assigned told you everything you needed to know about where the work stood.

AI has split those two functions apart in a way that the assignee field cannot handle. When a developer opens their AI coding assistant and describes a feature, the AI generates the implementation. The developer reviews it, adjusts it, tests it, and ships it. Who did the work? The answer is genuinely complicated. The developer directed the work, made the key decisions, caught the errors, and takes responsibility for the outcome. But the AI wrote most of the code, often drafted the tests, and in some cases produced the documentation as well. A git blame on that code will show the developer's name, because they committed it. But attributing the work to them in the way the assignee field implies, as if they sat down and wrote it line by line, is a fiction that everyone on the team is quietly aware of.

This is not a problem unique to junior developers or to trivial tasks. Senior engineers at the most sophisticated software companies in the world are using AI tools to write code they could not have written as quickly, or sometimes at all, without AI assistance. The question of who wrote the code is increasingly not a question that has a clean human answer, and a ticketing system designed around the assumption that it does will produce misleading data.

**The deeper issue is what assigning work actually does to the flow of that work.** When a ticket is assigned to a specific person, it enters their personal queue. It waits for them, specifically, regardless of whether other team members are available and capable. If that person is overloaded, the ticket waits. If they're in meetings, the ticket waits. If they're uncertain about the scope and need to ask a question, the ticket waits. The assignment creates a dependency not on the availability of capacity in general, but on the availability of one particular person.

Operations researchers call this a dedication queue, and it is well understood in systems design that dedication queues are less efficient than shared queues when work items are roughly interchangeable.[^1] A bank that assigns each customer to a specific teller will have longer average wait times than a bank with a single line feeding any available teller, even if the two banks have identical total capacity. The same principle applies to software work. When tasks are assigned to individuals rather than pulled from a shared pool, the system's throughput is limited by the availability of specific people rather than the availability of the team as a whole.

EASY's answer to this is not a new coordination mechanism. It is the removal of the mechanism entirely. In a system with no assignees, work is pulled by whoever is available and capable, in the same way that a team of surgeons pulls the next patient from the surgical schedule rather than waiting for a specific surgeon to become free. The work moves when capacity is available. No ticket sits idle because its named owner is in a meeting.

The accountability that the assignee field was supposedly providing does not disappear when you remove it. It transfers to the team as a whole, which is where it was always most honestly located anyway. A team that ships features is accountable for those features, regardless of which team member's name is on which ticket. A team that doesn't ship is failing collectively, and pointing to individual ticket owners rarely explains why. **The assignee field gives accountability the appearance of precision while distributing its actual weight unevenly and inefficiently.**

There are three questions worth addressing directly, because they come up in almost every conversation about removing assignees: 

- If no one owns the work, who answers questions about it? 
- Who presents it in the sprint review? 
- Who is on the hook if it breaks in production?

The answer is that these are questions about communication and on-call responsibility, not about ticket ownership. They are solved by documentation, by code review practices, by on-call rotations, and by a team culture where everyone is expected to understand the work well enough to speak to it. These are harder to solve than adding a name to a field in Jira, which is partly why the assignee field became a substitute for solving them properly. Removing it creates a small productive pressure to build the practices that should have been there anyway.

In a world where AI is generating significant portions of every codebase, the question of who wrote the code will progressively become less answerable and less relevant. What will remain relevant is whether the code works, whether the team understands it, and whether the organization is shipping. Those questions are answered by running the software, not by reading ticket metadata.
