# Chapter 8: One Question

Part One was a long argument for a short conclusion. Strip away the story points, the sprints, the velocity metrics, the assignees, the due dates, the ceremonies, and the dashboards, and what you are left with is a question that has existed since the first person ever delegated work to another person: is it done?

This is the foundation of EASY. Not a framework, not a process, not a set of ceremonies. A question. Everything else that follows in this part of the book is an explanation of how to organize work around that question as efficiently as possible, but the question itself is the whole of it. If you understand why this question is the right one, you understand the methodology.

*Why This Question Survives*

**The question survives every change in how work gets done because it is not about how the work gets done.** It is about whether the desired state of the world has been achieved. A feature either exists in the product or it does not. A bug is fixed or it is not. Documentation is written or it is not. These are binary facts about the world, and they remain binary regardless of whether a human wrote the code, an AI wrote it, a team of five wrote it together, or a single developer wrote it alone at two in the morning. The mechanism of production is irrelevant to the answer.

This is precisely why the question survives the AI transition when everything else does not. Story points measure human effort. Velocity measures human throughput. Capacity planning models human availability. Assignees track human labor. All of these practices are about the mechanism of production, and the mechanism has changed in ways that make those practices unreliable. But "is it done" is indifferent to mechanism. It was a useful question before Agile existed, and it will be a useful question for whatever comes after AI-assisted development. It is the one constant in the system.

*What AI Cannot Answer*

There is a second reason this question is the right foundation, and it is less obvious but equally important: it is a question that AI cannot answer for you. This matters more than it might initially seem.

AI can generate code. It can write documentation. It can draft requirements and produce test cases and summarize pull requests. As AI capability continues to improve, the list of things it can do will expand in ways that are difficult to predict. But there is one thing AI cannot do, which is decide whether the thing you wanted to build is the thing that was built. That judgment requires a human being with knowledge of the intent behind the work, the context in which it will be used, and the standards against which it should be evaluated. Done is not a computation. It is a judgment. And judgment, for the foreseeable future, belongs to people.

**EASY is structured around the one judgment that cannot be automated.** Everything else in a software team's workflow can be augmented, accelerated, or eventually replaced by AI tools. The Done judgment cannot, which means it is the one judgment worth building a system around.

It is also worth being clear about what the question does not ask. It does not ask:

- Who did the work
- How long the work took
- How difficult the work was
- How closely reality matched the estimate. 

It asks only whether the state of the world has changed in the way that was intended. This is not a limitation of the methodology, but rather a deliberate choice to track only the information that is both necessary and reliable, and to discard everything else.

Organizations that are early to adopt this framing will find that it compounds in unexpected ways. When you stop tracking who did the work, you stop creating the social dynamics around credit and blame that slow teams down. When you stop tracking how long things took, you stop anchoring expectations to historical performance that may no longer be relevant. When you stop tracking estimates versus actuals, you stop maintaining the fiction of predictability and start dealing honestly with uncertainty. Each of these changes removes a source of friction that teams rarely notice until it is gone.

The chapters that follow explain how to build the minimal system that this question requires. It is a short explanation, because the system is simple. That simplicity is not a feature that will be added later or a limitation waiting to be overcome, but rather the point of the system itself. The question is simple, and the system is built to answer it as directly as possible.
